The Story

On a cool October morning in 1828, in a Pennsylvania borough barely eight months old, a bank opened its doors. The Miners Bank, they called it — a name that honored the men who were carving a fortune from the anthracite seams of Broad Mountain and the hills of the Schuylkill Valley.

For a century, the bank grew alongside Pottsville. It predated Yuengling, the brewery that would become America’s oldest. It survived panics, depressions, and the slow transformation of a coal-powered nation into something new. Through it all, it remained the financial bedrock of a region.

Then came the centennial. The year was 1927, and the bank’s directors decided to celebrate one hundred years with a building worthy of the occasion. They turned to Cass Gilbert — the architect who had given New York the Woolworth Building, then the tallest in the world, and who would go on to design the United States Supreme Court. What Gilbert created in Pottsville was neither skyscraper nor courthouse, but something equally remarkable: a cathedral of commerce, scaled to a small city but built with the materials and ambition of a nation’s capital.

The exterior rose in Harvard brick, its facade anchored by four Doric columns hewn from white Georgia marble. Above the tall arched windows, carved keystones bore the faces of Mercury, god of trade, and Pluto, god of the deep earth — the twin spirits of a mining city. At the entrance, sculptor John Polachek cast ten-foot brass doors depicting miners at their labor. His work would later be collected by the Smithsonian. The doors cost ten thousand dollars — at a time when that sum could buy a house.

Inside, the building was even more extraordinary. The banking hall rose fifty feet to a vaulted ceiling crowned with a glass and bronze skylight. Twelve chandeliers hung from above, flooding the room with warm light. The walls were clad in Jeanne d’Arc stone, a French limestone chosen for its subtle variation of color. The floors were laid with Roman travertine, quarried from the same Italian hillsides that had supplied the builders of the Colosseum two thousand years earlier. The counters were American black walnut, their surfaces topped with slabs of dark green marble. In all, fourteen distinct types of marble were used throughout the building. And behind the tellers stood the vault: a door weighing thirty-six tons, twenty-seven inches of tool-proof steel, secured by twenty-four bolts and four time locks.

The building served as the financial heart of Pottsville for nearly a century. Banks merged, names changed — Miners National became Heritage National, then Sovereign, then Santander. When the last bank closed its doors in 2023, the future of Cass Gilbert’s masterpiece was uncertain.

It is uncertain no longer. The Miners 1928 is a careful, loving restoration of every surface, every detail, every material that Gilbert specified nearly a century ago — transformed into a luxury hotel and event destination for a new era. The travertine floors shine again. The skylight floods the grand hall with light once more. And those brass doors still open onto something extraordinary.
